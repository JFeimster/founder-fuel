Credit Stacking

A personal-credit-led strategy used to assemble multiple credit lines or 0% cards to create startup runway when the business is too early for conventional lending.

  • Personal credit is strong and recent utilization is controlled.
  • The business has a specific use of funds with a payoff timeline.
  • The founder understands repayment pressure and cash discipline.

Best for startups with real sales momentumrevenue based financing for startups

Revenue-Based Financing

A financing path tied to revenue performance, often used by ecommerce or service businesses with consistent deposits, processor data, and a clear growth use case.

  • Consistent revenue and processor or bank statement history
  • A use of funds connected to measurable growth
  • Gross margin strong enough to absorb repayment drag

Microloans + Grants

Smaller-check funding paths that can work earlier than banks for founders with limited history, especially when paired with local programs, incubators, or mission-aligned support.

  • Early-stage founder with light or inconsistent revenue
  • Business can document use of funds clearly

FAQ

Questions founders ask when the bank door stays shut.

Can a startup get business funding with no revenue?

Sometimes, but usually not through traditional banks. Early-stage founders with no revenue are more likely to rely on grants, crowdfunding, carefully managed personal-credit-led strategies, or smaller ecosystem-backed programs.

Are business loans for new businesses realistic?

They can be, but it depends on how new, how documented, and how risky the profile looks. Many founders hear 'business loan' and imagine a bank product that is actually designed for more mature businesses.

What is credit stacking for startups?

Credit stacking is a strategy that uses multiple personal-credit-based lines or cards to create startup runway. It can be useful, but it is not risk-free and it requires disciplined management.

When does revenue-based financing make sense?

It tends to make more sense for businesses with visible, consistent revenue and a strong use-of-funds case. It makes less sense when margins are thin or the founder is trying to solve a broken offer with expensive capital.

Do microloans and grants matter if the amounts are smaller?

Yes. Smaller capital can still be strategically important when the goal is proving discipline, building operating history, or financing a clear next step without taking on an oversized repayment burden.
